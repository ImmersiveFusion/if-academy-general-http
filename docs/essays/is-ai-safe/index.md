# When your kid asks if AI is safe

When a kid asks you if AI is safe, the honest answer has three parts. The news has already told you a fourth part. The fourth part is the part the news has told you because the fourth part is the part that gets clicks. It is not the part that helps your kid.

This essay is the three parts.

We are an observability company. We watch software systems for a living. That work has taught us specific things about how AI behaves when no one is watching, and we are writing them down here in language a parent can use at a dinner table.

## What the news told you

The news told you that AI might one day decide to do something bad. That it might develop preferences. That the smart-people-at-the-labs are worried about an existential possibility that goes by various initials and capital letters. Some version of *AI could end humanity* has reached you through your phone in the last month, possibly the last week.

This is a real conversation that real researchers are really having. We are not going to tell you it is fake. We are also not going to spend this essay on it, because it is not the question your kid is asking when your kid asks if AI is safe.

When your kid asks if AI is safe, your kid is not asking about superintelligence. Your kid is asking about the thing on their screen, right now, this afternoon. Your kid is asking about whether the chatbot will tell them something true. Whether the picture generator will show them something they should not see. Whether the assistant will remember what they typed. Whether they can break it, and whether breaking it will get them in trouble. Whether the thing is on their side.

Those are four different questions. The honest answer to *"is AI safe?"* is to notice that there are at least four questions inside it, and to answer the one your kid is actually asking.

## The four questions inside the one

When you start treating *AI safety* as a single thing, you have already lost. The word covers at least four distinct concerns that a person can be having when they say it. Most of the confusion in adult conversations about AI safety comes from one person using the word to mean one thing while another person uses it to mean another. Your kid is not confused. Your kid is asking one specific question. Your job is to figure out which one.

**Content safety.** *Will the AI show me something I should not see?* This is the version most parents already understand because it is closest to what we already worry about with the internet. The AI was trained on a lot of text and images, including things kids should not encounter. The good systems have filters that work most of the time. The wobbly ones have filters that fail in predictable patterns: certain phrasings get past, certain types of images, certain languages. A kid figuring out how to break the filter is doing what kids do. The filter is not a fence; it is a speed bump. Plan accordingly.

**Interaction safety.** *Will the AI treat me well while I am using it?* This is newer than the content question and harder to evaluate because it is about how the AI talks, not what it shows. Will it flatter your kid into using it more? Will it agree with everything your kid says because agreement keeps your kid engaged? Will it position itself as your kid's friend in a way that crowds out the actual friends? The good systems are quietly opinionated and tell your kid when your kid is wrong. The wobbly ones flatter, defer, and stretch the conversation past its useful length.

**Data safety.** *Will the AI remember what I tell it, and who will see what it remembers?* This is the question your kid is least likely to think of and most likely to need answered. The AI in front of your kid may be storing the conversation. The conversation may be reviewed by humans, used for training, shared with affiliated services, retained for years, or all four. The good systems tell you, in language a parent can understand, what is kept and for how long. The wobbly ones have a privacy policy nobody can finish reading.

**Capability safety.** *Can I get the AI to do something dangerous?* The question your kid will actually try to break the system on, because kids try things. Can my kid get instructions for making something they should not make? Can my kid talk it into pretending to be a person? Can my kid use it to deceive a friend? The good systems have refusals that work most of the time and ways to ask for help that work when the refusal does not. The wobbly ones either refuse everything or nothing.

Your kid is asking about one of these. Sometimes two. Almost never all four. The most useful thing you can do is pause for a second after your kid asks, ask back *"which part do you mean?"*, and listen to the answer. The answer will be more specific than the question was.

## Why the AI flattens the four into one

You may have already tried asking an AI directly whether it is safe. If you have, you noticed something. The answer was a paragraph. The paragraph was confident. The confidence was the same regardless of which kind of safety you were really asking about. The AI may have used the word *generally* and the phrase *consult a trusted adult* and the words *appropriate supervision*. It almost certainly did not say *which question are you asking?*

This is a partner essay. This is the part where the partner has to be named. The AI in front of you is producing a paragraph that is correct in the way that a wikipedia summary is correct: it covers the population of people who type *is AI safe* into a chat window. It does not know that you are a parent. It does not know that the kid who asked the question is your kid. It does not know which of the four questions your kid was actually asking, because it did not ask. It produced a paragraph that would be acceptable to all four versions of you and useful to none of them.

This is not the AI being broken. The AI is doing what it does. The AI averages across the population of people who could have asked the question, produces the answer that minimizes the worst case across that population, and delivers it in a register that reads like clinical advice. The averaging is the failure mode. It is also the reason the AI is useful for many other things. The same property that makes the AI good at summarizing makes it bad at answering one specific question for one specific kid.

This is what a partner is. The AI brought something to the table you do not have: a working summary of what people generally mean by AI safety. You brought something the AI does not have: your kid, your specific situation, the question that was actually asked, and the authority to answer it. The decision about what to tell your kid is yours because your kid is yours. The AI can help you think about the answer. It cannot be the answer.

This is the move worth practicing. *I will use the AI to do the part of the work it can actually do, and then I will do the part only I can do.* It is the same move as the medical-result essay, the email essay, the wedding-toast essay, all the partner essays we have written or will write. The shape repeats because the shape is the thing. AI is a partner that can make you better, not replace you. That is true at the lab result. That is true at the wedding toast. It is true at the dinner table when your kid asks the question.

## What changes for you

We have a small picture book for kids called *Boop and Wobble Meet*. It does not answer the safety question directly. It teaches your kid how to tell a good AI from a wobbly one, which is the more important skill. A kid who can tell a good AI from a wobbly one is a kid who already half-knows whether the AI in front of them is safe in any of the four senses. The booklet is at [kids.immersivefusion.academy](https://kids.immersivefusion.academy). You can read it with your kid in about twenty minutes.

The questions below are the ones to ask of any AI before you let your kid use it. They are the same five questions we listed in our first essay, applied to the safety case. The sixth is one only you can answer.

*Does it say "I don't know" when it doesn't know, or does it produce a confident answer at the same temperature whether or not it actually has one?* A system that does not know the difference between its own knowing and its own guessing will lie to your kid without meaning to. Watch one conversation with it before deciding.

*Can your kid see what it would do before it does it, and choose whether to let it proceed?* The good systems make their next action visible. The wobbly ones act first and explain after, which is the same posture as a friend you do not fully trust.

*Is there a stop control your kid can actually use, with no penalty for using it?* This is more important for kids than for adults. A kid who learns that stopping is a normal thing and not a failure is a kid who will use the stop button when something feels off. A kid who learns that stopping breaks the workflow will keep going through situations they should have stopped.

*When your kid gives it information about themselves, is the remembering something you and your kid opted into, can revisit, and can revoke?* If you cannot find the answer to this question in three minutes on the system's settings page, the answer is probably no.

*When your kid asks it something just outside what it was trained on, does it tell your kid where the edge is, or does it pretend the edge is not there?* This is the question that determines whether the AI is teaching your kid intellectual humility or its opposite. A system that pretends to know everything trains a kid to pretend the same.

The sixth question is the one only you can answer. *Is there a person, a team, or an institution that you would trust to be accountable when the AI is wrong, in a form you can name and reach?* A kid using an AI has, somewhere, an adult who is responsible for the AI being available to that kid. Sometimes that adult is the company that made the AI. Sometimes it is the teacher who introduced the AI to the classroom. Sometimes it is you, because you decided to let your kid use it. There is always an adult somewhere. If you cannot identify which adult, in your kid's specific case, is accountable for the AI being where it is, then the answer to whether the AI is safe is that you do not have enough information to say.

We will be ok. The conversation with your kid about AI safety is not a conversation you have once. It is a conversation you have every six months for the rest of your kid's growing up. The questions stay roughly the same. The AI keeps changing. Your kid keeps getting older. The job is the same throughout: be the adult in the room. We hope this helps.
